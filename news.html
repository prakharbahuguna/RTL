Autumn News 2014

Corrosion Monitoring Systems

RTL have just shipped another full thermal and corrosion scanner to South Korea. This is the sixth scanner to be installed in this country and the second on this modern 'supercritical powered' station that provides electricity for the capital, Seoul. The scanner comes complete with the latest monitoring software and analysis package which sends on-line thermal and corrosion data to the plant control room.

A thermal only scanner extension has been supplied to Drax power station for their Unit 4 boiler. This greatly increases the capability of the existing thermal scanner so that combustion conditions that result in tube rupture can be successfully monitored and eventually overcome.

The stand-alone non-intrusive heat flux and corrosion monitors are now in their final stage of development. The first production boards are being produced and these should be ready for full testing in a power station in September. These new computer controlled stand-alone monitoring systems have application in many other areas apart from combustion systems, such as pipelines, vessels and industrial plant where there is a risk from corrosion.

Corrosion and Cathodic Protection Systems

RTL have recently completed the design and installation of cathodic protection systems to a church tower in Shropshire and to 18th century muses at Seaton Delaval Hall – a National Trust property. The church bellcote contains a wrought iron ring beam embedded in the masonry and also iron cores to the columns, all of which were corroding and cracking the historic stones. Seaton Delaval Hall contains six muses with wrought iron armatures, all of which have been suffering from ongoing corrosion which had cracked and delaminated the plaster/lime bodies and limbs of the sculptures. This is the first time that RTL have applied cathodic protection to plaster and lime statues.

RTL have been involved with the historic concrete Tecton buildings at Dudley Zoo for the past two years. These buildings are currently being repaired and conserved using techniques compiled by RTL over the past 10 years. A technical paper outlining the repair and conservation of historic concrete has been submitted to the Journal of Architectural Conservation.

The system monitors all four boiler walls and incorporates the latest on- line data analysis software which gives both thermal and corrosion rate maps of the boiler walls. RTL's R&D division has recently developed a stand-alone non-intrusive heat flux sensor that makes use of combination of novel measurement techniques. These new low-cost systems are intended to compete against the existing intrusive heat flux probes which are used to activate soot blower and water cannon operations. These devices should be commercially available in spring 2014.

Autumn News 2013

Corrosion Monitoring Systems

RTL have recently completed a contract to supply a full corrosion scanner to the sidewalls of the Unit 6 boiler at Drax Power Station – the third Drax boiler to be fully instrumented with a corrosion monitoring system installed on the sidewalls. The coal-fired boilers are increasingly having lower- NOx systems installed which results in increasingly 'reducing conditions' within the combustion zones. This can result in high levels of corrosion so careful control of the stoichiometry is essential.

The company has now automated the corrosion rate analysis package and on-line corrosion rate, metal loss and remaining thickness maps are continually being output from the data loggers. The thermal and corrosion rate data is fed back to the OIS via a fibre optic link so that the boiler operators have direct access to the data.

RTL have also installed a fast thermal scanner on a section of the back wall of the Unit 4 boiler. This area has increasingly suffered from localised thermal tube damage which have resulted in unscheduled outages. The non-intrusive sensors are used to give estimated fireside information (front face temperatures and heat flux values) and this is allowing the engineers to investigate when (and how) these transient conditions occur.

The company has recently been awarded another contract in Korea – to supply a full corrosion scanner to the Unit 3 boiler at Samcheonpo Power Station. This is the third boiler to be fitted out at this site.

Spring News 2013

Corrosion Monitoring Systems

RTL successfully completed a corrosion monitoring programme during biofuel trials carried out on the Unit 1 boiler at Drax Power station in 2011/12. The ground breaking work involved monitoring both the corrosion of the carbon steel furnace walls and also localised attack of the austenitic stainless steel used for the superheaters and reheaters of the 660MW boiler. The high level of alkaline metals in some of the biofuels can result in substantial corrosion of stainless steels, and this was confirmed when some of the worst fuels were combusted and corrosion rates recorded unacceptable levels. The on-line monitoring allowed corrosion rates to be confirmed within around 24 hours.

RTL has subsequently been asked to provide a complete monitoring package for the Unit 2 boiler which will be converted to 100% biofuel firing in March 2013. The corrosion scanner system will be the largest ever produced by the company and will take measurements for the full height of both sidewalls and with four superheater probes in the convective section to monitor the corrosion of both T11 and stainless steel Esshete 1250 alloy. The intention is that the boiler feed will initially be wood pellets, but it is possible that more corrosive fuels, such as agro-crops, may be fired later on. The outputs, comprising corrosion rates, metal loss, remaining metal thickness and heat flux values will be output to the DCS so that the boiler operators will have direct access to the information and can then take appropriate actions on boiler feed and combustion operation.

Autumn News 2012

Scanner Installations - South Korea

Three of the latest versions of our non-intrusive thermal and corrosion scanner systems are currently being installed on coal-fired power station boilers in South Korea. The latest scanner systems monitor both heat flux and corrosion on all four walls of these large boilers with real-time outputs (2D maps) being sent directly to the control rooms and also to the PI historian. The scanner will enable the power stations to optimise their combustion conditions and also allow lower quality coal to be fired in the safe knowledge that damage to the boilers is kept to a minimum.

Power Plant Corrosion Monitoring - UK

Rowan Technologies is continuing its corrosion monitoring programme in the superheater section of a 660MW coal-fired boiler in the UK. The two air-cooled variable temprature electrical resistance (VTER) probes monitor corrosion associated with biomass firing.
